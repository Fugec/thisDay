Overview

The Hijrah was Muhammad’s migration and that of his followers from Mecca to Yathrib (now Medina) in 622 CE. It marked a turning point in Islamic history, as Muslims fled religious persecution by the Quraysh and laid the foundation for an Islamic polity rooted in faith and social justice.

According to Islamic tradition, the journey began in secrecy. Muhammad and his companion Abu Bakr hid in the Cave of Thawr for three days to avoid pursuit. Guided by Abdallah ibn Arqat, they traveled by camel through harsh terrain and arrived safely in Medina by September 622.

The Hijrah is also considered the birth of the first organized Muslim community. Years later, Caliph Umar chose this migration as the epoch (starting point) of the Islamic calendar. Thus, 1 Muharram AH 1 ≈ 16 July 622 CE.

Conclusion

The Hijrah was far more than a physical migration—it was a monumental shift in the trajectory of human history. It signified the birth of an Islamic civilization built on principles of faith, unity, justice, and mutual respect. The move from Mecca to Medina allowed Muhammad not only to practice Islam freely, but to establish a functioning, pluralistic society governed by ethical law and communal harmony.

For Muslims worldwide, the Hijrah represents the beginning of a sacred timeline, but also a timeless moral lesson. It reminds believers that spiritual progress often requires courage, endurance, and trust in divine guidance. From the shadows of persecution in Mecca to the dawn of a thriving community in Medina, the Hijrah remains a beacon of hope and transformation.

As we reflect on this pivotal event over 1,400 years later, its message still resonates: that when values are rooted in justice and compassion, even the most difficult journeys can lead to extraordinary beginnings.
